Why Does Washington State See Hundreds of Vehicle Crashes Every Day?

By: Jeffrey Choi | June 4, 2026

Image Source: KOMO News/WSDOT (May 30, 2026)


Last year, Washington had a reported 100,569 vehicle crashes, which is roughly 275 every day. According to the Washington State Department of Transportation Crash Data, this statistic has barely fluctuated, with 100,000 crashes continuing to occur each year since 2015, which is as far as the data is available from. Although the total has barely changed, the crash causes from driving behaviors and who is involved are continuing to shift over time. Despite constant road infrastructure construction and advances in vehicle technology, traveling on roads remains a daily health and life risk for everyone. Even when following safe driving policies, many people are victims of the dangerous decisions of others. Improving the safety of our roads is a community effort that requires improving our personal driving habits, increasing awareness, and urging policymakers and planners to prevent risks and improve law enforcement.

Current driving laws

Washington enforces many driving behavior laws to prevent crashes, with major criminal charges beyond minor traffic violations. These are listed for clear understanding on the Washington State Patrol’s Rules of the Road site. Some include reckless driving, distracted driving, racing, and aggressive driving, with charges escalating based on the harm of the incidents. It is also unlawful to carry open alcohol or be drinking in a vehicle on the road, and alcohol concentration is limited to under 0.08%. For drugs, there is a legal THC concentration limit of 5.00, and potential arrests for drivers showing any signs of intoxication. Drivers under the age of 21 and Commercial Driver License holders are subject to more zero-tolerance policies. Even with these laws in place, people still engage in dangerous behaviors due to poor judgment, intentional law-breaking, and underestimating the risks and consequences for doing so.

Crash causes in the last decade

There is a wide variety of reasons for vehicle crashes, but a few categories have remained the most common driving behaviors causing deadly crashes. This chart shows the last ten years of fatal crashes in Washington for distracted driving, speeding, intoxicated driving, and fatigued driving due to lack of sleep.

WA Fatal Vehicle Crash Causes 2015-2025

Speeding and being impaired by alcohol are both consistent driving behavior issues with legal consequences. In 2020, distracted driving saw a major decrease to below 20% of the total crashes. According to the Washington Traffic Safety Commission , this drop was due to a change in how officers reported distracted driving and has not been updated to properly reflect it in the data. This boosted the distribution of other causes, but a major increase was drug usage, likely caused by isolation from the COVID pandemic. Other crash causes not included in this data are aggressive driving or road rage, as well as weather or road conditions.

The most dangerous roads and drivers

Crash risk is present everywhere in Washington, but it is most present in highly populated cities with dense traffic. In 2025, King County had the most crashes of 30.5k, while the surrounding Snohomish and Pierce each had around 12k. These add up to over half of the state’s crashes and highlight how the population density and highway usage contribute to the highest risks. Other high-crash counties include Spokane to the east and Clark to the south, which contains Vancouver city near Portland. To see more specific crash counts, hovering over the map will display each county’s numbers for 2025.

Crash consequences

Last year, 664 people died from vehicle crashes in Washington, and an additional 2,985 people received serious injuries. Drivers themselves experience the majority of these, but pedestrians and motorcyclists have high fatalities in comparison to the serious injuries because of their lack of protection against vehicles that weigh thousands of pounds.

2025 WA Vehicle Fatalities and Serious Injuries

In this chart, young drivers and old drivers are separated to show how their lack of experience and poor health in old age compare. Even though the rest of the drivers, from ages 26 to 64, make up the majority of daily traffic, young and old drivers have significantly high fatalities. Based on the ratio of fatalities to serious injuries, older people are more likely to die than survive with injuries due to their health. A high number of passengers were also affected by crashes, even though they were not the ones in control of the vehicles. The consequences of unsafe driving are beyond just the drivers themselves, with primarily innocent people being killed for the irresponsible actions of others.

What can we do to improve road safety?

Improving road safety and decreasing the amount of yearly deaths will be impacted by more law enforcement, infrastructure changes, and education. But more importantly, it requires reflecting on our own behavior on roads, even when we are crossing a street. Every person who breaks the law will not be arrested or pulled over, but that should not stop people from trying to actively prevent deaths and injuries by following the policies. All roads have the potential danger of crashing a vehicle, especially in cities with daily commuting and intersections. With vehicle crashes remaining a common cause of death, the consequences are not worth the distractions or impairments that are avoidable. It is a shared responsibility to make our local roads safer with our daily actions and support for future prevention.
